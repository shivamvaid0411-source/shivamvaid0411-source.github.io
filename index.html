How I spend my soul

Writing
— The Chai That Never Cooled

There was a tea stall on the corner of our lane that I used to stop at every morning before school. The owner — a man with a permanent squint and flour on his shirt — never asked what I wanted. He just started making it when he saw me coming.

One chai. Two sugars. No conversation needed.

I left that city three years ago. But some mornings, when the light hits a certain way and the air smells like something you can't name, I'm back at that corner. Waiting for a cup that's already ready. Standing in a life I didn't know I'd miss.

I think that's the thing about home — you don't realize it was home until you're somewhere that almost feels like it.

Eating across India
I don't travel to see monuments. I travel to eat. Every city has a street, every street has a cart, every cart has a guy who makes one thing perfectly. Finding that guy — that's the whole trip.

Chole bhature in Delhi. Misal pav in Pune. Kachori in Varanasi. The list keeps growing. The jeans keep shrinking.
🧭
Guiding through the fog
When someone's stuck in their head at 2am — spiralling, overthinking, falling apart — I'm the person they call. Not because I have answers, but because I know how to sit with someone in the dark without rushing them toward the light.

Emotional crises. Existential questions. Life feeling too big. I've been there. I can hold the space.
